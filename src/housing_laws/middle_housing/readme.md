# Middle Housing

<details>
<summary>Housing Law Summary</summary>
<p>

ORS 197A.420 requires certain local governments to allow middle housing on residential lands that allow for the development of detached single-unit dwellings. “Middle housing” includes the following: 

- **Duplex:** two attached or detached dwellings in any configuration on a lot or parcel

- **Triplex:** three attached or detached dwellings in any configuration on a lot or parcel

- **Quadplex:** four attached or detached dwellings in any configuration on a lot or parcel

- **Townhouse:** a dwelling unit constructed in a row of two or more attached units, where each dwelling unit is located on an individual lot or parcel and shares at least one common wall with an adjacent unit.

- **Cottage cluster:** a grouping of dwelling units that are detached or attached in subgroupings of up to four units in any configuration, that have a common courtyard, and that each have a small footprint or floor area.

- **Bonus affordable or accessible middle housing units** allowed under ORS 197A.421

- **Existing retained units** allowed under ORS 197A.420(4)

Middle housing allowances vary depending on the population and geographic characteristics of affected local governments.

### Zoned for Residential Use

The law requires allowing middle housing on each lot or parcel that is “zoned for residential use”, which means land that:

- Is within the urban growth boundary,

- Has base zoning for, or is designated to allow residential uses,

- Allows the development of a detached single-unit dwelling

- Is not zoned primarily for commercial, industrial, agricultural or public uses, and

- Is incorporated or urban unincorporated land.

For land to be eligible, it must meet all five criteria. This generally includes urban residential zones that allow for the development of detached single-unit dwellings.

### Duplexes in ‘Medium Cities’

Cities that are outside of the Portland Metro with a population of 2,500 or greater and less than 25,000 must allow a duplex on each lot or parcel zoned for residential use. Cities in this population range are referred to as “medium cities”. These cities are not required to allow other middle housing types. 

### All Middle Housing in ‘Large Cities’

A local government meeting any of the following must allow all middle housing types on each lot or parcel zoned for residential use. These local governments are referred to as “large cities”:

-	A city outside of the Portland Metro with a population greater than 25,000,

-	A city with a population of 1,000 or greater within the Portland Metro,

-	A city within Tillamook County,

-	A county with jurisdiction over urban unincorporated land, or

- The following unincorporated communities within Tillamook County: Barview/Twin Rocks/Watseco, Cloverdale, Hebo, Neahkahnie, Neskowin, Netarts, Oceanside and Pacific City/Woods

### Existing, Retained Units

Medium and large cities must allow a lot or parcel with an existing single-unit dwelling, single-unit dwelling plus accessory dwelling unit, or duplex to remain on the lot or parcel with a new middle housing development. The existing units may remain nonconforming with regard to siting and design standards – the city may only require the new units to meet siting and design standards.

### Siting and Design / Goal Protections

Medium and large cities may regulate the siting and design of middle housing allowed. However, applicable regulations may not create unreasonable cost or delay to middle housing development. Additionally, cities may apply goal protections to middle housing, such as those relating to natural resources or hazards.

Administrative rules (OAR Chapter 660, Division 046) outline more specifically how local governments may regulate siting and design and apply goal protections regulating the development of middle housing.

### Bonus Affordable or Accessible Units

ORS 197A.421 requires medium and large cities to allow additional bonus units for middle housing projects that include one or more accessible or affordable units. An “accessible unit” complies with “Type A” accessibility requirements in the state building code, and “affordable units” are units with an affordable housing covenant made available to purchase by a household with an income below 120 percent of median income.

The total number of bonus units that may be built depends on the middle housing being sited. For a duplex or triplex, projects can be eligible for up to one bonus unit (resulting in a triplex or quadplex). For a townhouse, quadplex, or cottage cluster, a development can qualify for up to two bonus units (resulting in a five- or six-unit development. Cities must also provide commensurate increases to the developable area, floor area, height or density requirements to allow the development of the units.
